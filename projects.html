Self-Driving Pacing Car

April 2017 - September 2018

We built a self driving robot to pace athletes around a running track.

As competitive track runners, my friends and I wanted a robot that could guide runners through perfectly-paced lap times. As a team of 5, we built a small electric car, and taught it how to drive itself around a 400m track.
Technical Skills: We developed our own navigation system, allowing the car to accurately stay between running lanes while driving at high speeds through any outdoor weather conditions. We used a PIXY-cam camera for object/line detection, and we integrated it with infrared sensors, LCD, keypad, motor driver and a temperature sensor. We used data from the camera and infrared sensors to develop a PID navigation system, enabling the car to get back on track even if it lost the line it was following.
Project Management Skills: The project was also a fun opportunity to practice defining project plans and timelines, managing finances and establishing systems for ordering components. Working with physics and engineering students, it was rewarding to see all our skillsets compound to bring our dream to life.


UBC Rocket Engineering Design Team

April 2017 - September 2019

We build super-sonic rockets to compete in international competitions, carrying on-board experiments above 30’000ft.

UBC Rocket is a fast-paced student engineering design team at the University of British Columbia. With over 500 applicants to the team every year, the team is extremely competitive. As a kid I dreamt of being a rocket scientist, so in university I decided to dedicate my time outside of class to the Rocket team.
Throughout my 2+ years on the team, I worked in many areas of the team:

On-Board Experiments
I had the honour of leading the On-Board Experiments team in my first year on the team. Among other experiments, we built a muon detector to measure the rocket’s altitude in flight. Cosmic rays are constantly bombarding our atmosphere, produced by supernovas and other fiery explosion in the universe. When these cosmic rays hit our atmosphere, they decay into a slew of particles, including muons! The higher you are, the more muons there are flying around. Muons last only a fraction of a second, but we can catch them with a special material that emits a very faint flash of light when a muon crashes into it. We count the flashes of light, and calculate the rate at which muons are hitting our device. The idea was to use the rate of muon collisions to determine altitude, and test its accuracy as a novel altimeter technology. Unfortunately, the rocket carrying this experiment tore itself to shreds shortly after breaking the sound barrier, destroying all traces of our experiment :( . Liquid Propulsion Systems Development
In the interest of innovation, there is a division of the team designing and building a liquid propulsion system from scratch. (It’s a lot harder than it sounds). Liquid propulsion engines are a lot more powerful than solid engines, but they are also way more intricate, delicate, and dangerous. Working with a particle physics professor from UBC, we designed, simulated, tested, and began building our engine. At one point, we got to sit down with an aerospace engineer from the Canadian Space Agency as he validated our design (so cool!). I was responsible for parts & materials acquisition. This involved a lot of problem solving and critical thinking; it’s surprisingly hard for a 19-year-old kid to get his hands-on explosive fluids and high pressure plumbing. To make matters worse, as a student team we have an extremely limited budget, and our rockets tend to use around $30’000 in parts for each rocket. I became an expert at reaching out for sponsorships and finding low-cost alternatives to high tech solutions. My solutions included using high-performance paintball canisters to pressurize our engine, and collaborating with mechanical engineering students to manufacture our own components whenever possible.

Software Development & Avionics
Our rockets have computer systems to control each step of the rocket’s launch, flight, parachute deployments, and the on-board experiments. I developed software to transmit flight data from the rocket's computer to a user-friendly UI on a ground station. I learnt and applied the use of threads to ensure data protection and program speed. It was fun collaborating with hardware programmers, and optimising our software around the limitations of our hardware.

Virtual Reality for Single-Cell Data Exploration

October 2019 - HackSeq19 hackathon

Collaborated with Harvard researchers to visualize single-cell genomic data in virtual reality.

Genomic sequencing is getting so accurate that researchers can now precisely determine the DNA sequence of a single cell. By sequencing many cells individually, researchers can track the genetic differences between cells and between generations of cells. But how on earth would you visualize this? This is a new field of measurement, a mind-bending concept to visualize. During the HackSeq19 hackathon, I worked with researchers from Harvard to figure out how to visualize this data in an intuitive and insightful way. We brainstormed and tested many different concepts for this type of data visualization, and decided create a 3D graph that makes it easy to see defined cell lineages, as well as genetic outliers.

We used A-Frame as our VR framework, and the Harvard lab uses machine learning to group the cell genomes into lineages based on their DNA. The goal is for researchers to upload their own datasets, and our software will map the data into a 3D space, enabling the viewer to explore, interact, and “walkthrough” the different cell lineages. Placing heavy emphasis on user experience throughout our development, we made sure that the VR world was easy to navigate from a laptop, phone, or VR headset.

Our hackathon project was so successful, the Harvard lab published a paper based about it!
